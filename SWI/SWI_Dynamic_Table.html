SURFACE WATER ASSESSMENT

Consistency Analysis:
Within all parties in the Great Lakes region, state/provincial and local governments have developed SWAPs for all drinking water sources. There is less consistency at the federal level, as the Canadian government grants authority to the provinces to develop SWAPs but does not require them to do so.

The 1996 amendments to the Safe Drinking Water Act require the EPA to monitor, assess, and report potential threats to every public drinking water supply through Source Water Assessment Programs (SWAPs). These plans are developed regionally and are subject to EPA approval. EPA provides resources to assist states in developing their plans.
Click for More
EPA delegates the responsibility to develop SWAPs to the state regulatory agencies who work with municipalities and local governments to create the plans. State agencies then consult with EPA to gain approval for locally developed SWAPs. The Blue Accounting Source Water Compendium contains links to the SWAP websites for all Great Lakes States.
Click for More
Municipalities develop and carry out EPA-approved SWAPs in conjunction with state and regional governments. As of 2002, SWAPs have been approved and completed for all public water systems in the United States. Information on how to obtain regional SWAP data is contained in the Consumer Confidence Reports for each region.

The Canada Water Act grants authority to the provinces to develop water resource protection plans, including source water assessment plans, though they are not required. The Minister of Environment and Climate Change (MECC) may approve provincially developed plans. The Protocol for Centralized Drinking Water Systems in First Nations Communities does require First Nations to prepare SWAPs for on-reserve threats to raw water sources. The federal government provides resources for First Nations to use in developing these assessments.
Click for More
Ontario: The Ontario Clean Water Act requires source water assessments for all major watersheds in the province as part of the Drinking Water Source Protection Program. Ontario has designated 19 regional committees to develop assessments, representing each major watershed.
Quebec: The 2009 Quebec Water Act enhances the power of the province to implement SWAPs, designating 40 water management zones (OBVs). Each OBV is required to complete a Water Master Plan (PDE), which assesses threats to drinking water sources.
Click for More
Ontario: Representatives from local governments and municipalities make up regional source protection committees and work with the province to complete assessments. The 19 regional committees have developed 22 federally approved Source Water Protection Plans, which require assessment of threats to water quality.
Quebec: Representatives from each OBV work with stakeholders from local governments and municipalities to develop PDEs. PDEs (In French) for all 40 regions can be obtained through the website of each OBV, accessible through the Quebec Watershed Organization website.

SURFACE WATER PROTECTION

Consistency Analysis:
Both countries have no federal requirement to develop SWPPs. While Ontario requires SWPPs for all municipalities, no U.S. states have similar requirements. Quebec has developed a draft strategy for a province-wide SWPP, but it has yet to be implemented.

Click for More
The U.S. EPA heavily encourages local and state governments to use data from SWAPs to develop their own Source Water Protection Plans (SWPPs), though there is no requirement to do so. EPA provides resources, including possible federal funding sources, to state and local governments to help them develop SWPPs.
Click for More
Governments of several states encourage local governments to use data from SWAPs to develop their own SWPPs and a state regulatory agency may choose to approve a locally developed plan. Ohio has emerged as a leader in SWPPs among the Great Lakes states, with a growing list of municipalities that have an OEPA-endorsed plan.
Click for More

Several local governments have voluntarily developed their own SWPPs, many of which have been approved by the state regulatory agency. The Blue Accounting Source Water Compendium contains information on which local governments in the Great Lakes region have SWPPs.
Click for More

The Canada Water Act grants authority to the provinces to develop water resource protection plans, including SWPPs, though they are not required. The Minister of Environment and Climate Change (MECC) may approve provincially developed plans. The Protocol for Centralized Drinking Water Systems in First Nations Communities does require First Nations to prepare SWPPs for on-reserve threats to raw water sources. The federal government provides resources for First Nations to use in developing these plans.

Click for More
Ontario: The Ontario Clean Water Act requires development of SWPPs for all major watersheds in the province. Ontario has designated 19 regional committees to develop plans, representing each major watershed. Provincial agencies work with MECC to gain approval for regionally developed plans. Quebec: In 2012, Quebec completed its Draft Strategy for the Protection and Conservation of Drinking Water Sources; however, this plan has yet to be implemented. Each OBV has developed a Water Action Plan as a component of its PDE, which outlines voluntary strategies each region hopes to implement to protect source water bodies.
Click for More
Ontario: Representatives from local governments and municipalities make up regional source protection committees and work with the province of Ontario to design SWPPs. The 19 regional committees have developed 22 federally approved SWPPs, which include regionally-based policies to protect against specific contaminants. Quebec: Water Action Plans developed by each OBV outline strategies the region hopes to implement to protect source water bodies, but these actions are not enforceable by any specific policy at this time.
